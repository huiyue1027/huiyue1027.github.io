Interactive Experience Research

Course: Interactive Media

Jiashuo Liu

Researching: palat2023.com

1. Initial Attention

When I first loaded the site, the bold typography and the flashing neon colors really stood out. The pink and yellow background was so bright that it was impossible to look at anything else first. It felt like a visual explosion that immediately tells you this isn't a boring or traditional website.

2. Two-Minute Interaction

During my time on the site, I mostly tried to see what would react to my mouse. I started by scrolling down to see the different color sections, and then I spent time hovering over the text to see how it moves. I found a part with moving white squares and spent a while pushing them around. I also tried to click on the contact info at the bottom to see if I could actually grab the letters before they flew away.

3. Most Engaging Part

The part that kept me interested for the longest was the interactive pixel grid. It's really fun because the squares follow the physics of your mouse movement. It felt like playing a mini-game inside a website, and the feedback was so fast and smooth that I just kept doing it to see the different patterns I could create.

4. Most Common Action

The most common thing I did was moving my mouse around in circles or fast lines. Since almost every part of the site—from the text to the shapes—is tied to the mouse position, I found myself constantly waving the cursor just to trigger the animations and see what would happen next.

5. Primary Goal

I think the main goal of this website is to show that the developer is extremely creative and knows how to use advanced code. It's more of a showreel than a simple portfolio. It's meant to impress people by showing them a level of interactivity that they've probably never seen on a regular webpage before.

6. Goal Communication

The site shows this goal by being very "in your face" with its design. It doesn't just say it's creative; it makes you experience it. The way the interface breaks standard rules, like having text that moves away from you, shows a high level of confidence in creative coding.

7. Interaction Over Time

My impression is that you're supposed to visit this site once or twice for a short burst of time. It's designed to leave a very strong impact in five minutes. It's not something you would check every day for updates, but it's definitely something you would remember for a long time after just one visit.

8. Communicating Temporal Use

The single-page design really helps communicate this. It feels like a journey from top to bottom. Once you reach the contact links at the end and you've played with all the interactive sections, there isn't really anything else to do, which signals that the experience is complete.

9. Other Media References

The site reminds me a lot of 90s video games and pop art. The loud colors and the way things glitch out feel like old arcade machines. At the same time, the interactive parts remind me of modern art galleries where the art reacts to the people walking past it.

10. Influence on Action

These references made me feel like I didn't have to be serious while using the site. Because it looks like a game or a poster, I felt like I should play with it and explore the corners of the screen rather than just looking for a "Download CV" button like I usually do on portfolio sites.

11. Influence on Feeling

The design made me feel quite energized and curious. The bright colors and the "weird" movements of the text made me smile because it's so unexpected. It feels very personal and experimental, which makes the whole experience feel more fun than a normal school or work task.

12. Most Frustrating Part

The most frustrating thing was trying to actually use the links in the footer. The letters move away when you get close, which is a cool trick, but it makes it pretty hard to actually click the email or social media links. If I were actually trying to hire this person, I might find it a bit annoying to "chase" the text.

13. Most Satisfying Part

The most satisfying part was how responsive everything felt. There was no delay between my mouse moving and the screen reacting. That kind of high-speed feedback makes the website feel very high-quality and "expensive," even though the design itself looks quite raw and experimental.
